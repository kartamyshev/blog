An Alternative Design of the State

Feb 6, 2026

Most political systems today are built on an idea that quietly escapes scrutiny: power is treated as something special. Something elevated above normal rules of accountability. Politicians are elected, given long fixed terms, vague mandates, and then largely left alone. At best, they are judged years later. At worst, they are almost untouchable.

This is strange if you think about it.

In almost every other area of life, authority is conditional. Managers can be fired. Contractors lose contracts. Employees are evaluated continuously. If performance is bad, consequences are fast and procedural. No philosophy, no symbolism — just accountability.

Politics, for some reason, works the opposite way. Power Should Not Have Power. I believe political power should not be power at all. It should be management.

If people give a mandate, they should be able to take it back — not through extreme procedures like impeachment, but through ordinary, fast, technical mechanisms. A politician should be closer to a hired manager than to a semi-sacred figure.

Right now, political systems treat authority as something you “own” for four or five years. During that time, almost nothing can be done, even if it becomes obvious that the system is failing. In many countries, governments take months — sometimes half a year — just to form. Meanwhile, instability grows and responsibility dissolves.

In any normal job, this would be unacceptable.

Politics as a Corruption Interface

Let’s be blunt. In practice, politics has become the main interface for budget extraction. The scale varies by country, depending on how well the system is designed, but the pattern is universal.

This is not a moral argument. It’s a factual one.

Money can be tracked. Wars can be identified as initiated. Populism can be measured by promises versus outcomes. Abuse of power leaves transactional traces. These are not opinions. These are events.

Humans are bad at policing this at scale. Not because they are evil, but because corruption is a network phenomenon. People inside the system depend on each other. They negotiate, delay, reinterpret, and protect their own.

Machines don’t do that.

Machine learning systems don’t care who you are. They don’t negotiate. They don’t get tired. They don’t selectively look away. They are very good at one thing humans are consistently bad at: pattern detection across large systems.

From Promises to Executable Mandates

This is where the idea of contracts becomes unavoidable.

If a government program, a budget, or a political mandate were treated as an executable contract rather than a promise, the entire structure of power would change. Conditions would be explicit. Limits would be formal. Violations would be detectable automatically.

Smart contracts and similar technologies already allow this logic. Not as a magic solution, but as an enforcement layer. Funds can be locked. Spending can be conditional. Transgressions can trigger automatic freezes, audits, or removal.

The key shift is simple:
mandates stop being symbolic and become operational.
A politician would not “rule” for a fixed term. They would operate while conditions are met. The moment they are not, the system reacts — not emotionally, not politically, but procedurally.

Fixed Terms Are a Historical Artifacts

The idea that someone should govern for four, five, or six years regardless of performance is not a law of nature. It’s a historical compromise from a time when communication was slow, participation was limited, and monitoring was expensive.
None of that is true anymore.
In modern systems, long fixed terms mostly create moral hazard. Bad decisions can be delayed. Responsibility can be shifted. Failure can be explained away until the term ends.
In engineering, medicine, finance, or aviation, this would be insane.

Algorithms Don’t Replace Politics — They Replace Discretion

This is important: algorithms do not solve “values.” They don’t decide what is good or bad. They operate within defined rules.
But that’s exactly the point.
Most damage in politics doesn’t come from explicit ideology. It comes from discretionary power — from the ability to bend rules quietly, delay decisions indefinitely, or create exceptions without consequences.

Algorithms are very good at eliminating silent discretion. They can:

  • detect anomalies
  • enforce limits
  • log every action immutably
  • apply rules consistently

They don’t create justice. They create predictability.

The Myth of Objectivity

Of course, objectivity does not exist in a universal sense. Different societies have different norms. In some places, “objective morality” means tradition, hierarchy, and religious rules. In others, it means individual freedom and personal identity.

These frameworks change over time. Revolutions happen. What was once “objective” becomes unacceptable, and vice versa.
No system can compute the “correct” values. So the question is wrong. The real question is not what the values are, but how they change.

Procedure Over Truth

A stable system does not require universal agreement on values. It requires agreement on procedures.
Facts can be automated. Money stolen is a fact. A law broken is a fact. A war started is a fact.
Norms cannot be automated — but their evolution can be constrained.
A healthy system allows values to change:

  • slowly rather than violently,
  • transparently rather than covertly,
  • through broad consensus rather than force.

Algorithms can help here not by choosing values, but by:

  • showing who is affected by a rule,
  • measuring asymmetry of harm,
  • distinguishing between permission and coercion.

Freedom Is About Non-Participation

This leads to one of the most misunderstood points in modern politics. There is a fundamental difference between:

  • “Allow us to do X among ourselves” and
  • “Force everyone to live according to X.”

Freedom expands when people are allowed to opt in. Authoritarianism begins when opting out is punished.
This distinction matters more than whether a demand comes from a majority or a minority. History is full of cases where majorities used power to restrict basic freedoms. Numbers alone do not create legitimacy. The real line is coercion.

What Technology Can and Cannot Do

Technology cannot eliminate ideological conflict. It cannot define the meaning of life or the correct way to live. What it can do is far more modest — and far more powerful:

  • reduce corruption,
  • compress decision times,
  • eliminate silent abuse of power,
  • make authority conditional and revocable,
  • turn governance into an accountable service.

This is not utopia. It is system design.

A State as an Engineered System

The alternative design of the state is not anti-human and not anti-democratic. It is anti-mystical. Power becomes procedural. Authority becomes contractual. Politics becomes less about speeches and more about execution. Not perfect. Not final. But significantly harder to abuse. And maybe that’s the most realistic improvement we can aim for.
